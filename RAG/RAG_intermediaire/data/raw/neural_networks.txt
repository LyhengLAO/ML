# Neural Networks and Deep Learning

A neural network is a model composed of layers of interconnected units called
neurons. Each neuron computes a weighted sum of its inputs, adds a bias, and
passes the result through a non-linear activation function such as ReLU, sigmoid,
or tanh. The non-linearity is what allows networks to approximate complex
functions.

Deep learning refers to neural networks with many hidden layers. Training is
performed using backpropagation, which computes the gradient of a loss function
with respect to every weight using the chain rule. An optimizer such as
stochastic gradient descent or Adam then updates the weights to reduce the loss.

Convolutional neural networks, or CNNs, are specialized for grid-like data such
as images. They use convolutional filters to detect local patterns like edges
and textures, and pooling layers to reduce spatial resolution. Recurrent neural
networks, or RNNs, were designed for sequential data, but they struggle with
long-range dependencies because of vanishing gradients. Long Short-Term Memory
networks, or LSTMs, partially solve this with gating mechanisms.

Regularization methods for neural networks include dropout, which randomly
deactivates neurons during training, and batch normalization, which stabilizes
and accelerates training by normalizing layer activations. The learning rate is
one of the most important hyperparameters: too high and training diverges, too
low and training is painfully slow.
