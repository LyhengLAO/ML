# Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG, is a technique that connects a large
language model to an external knowledge source. Instead of relying only on what
the model memorized during training, RAG retrieves relevant documents at query
time and inserts them into the prompt as context. This grounds the answer in real
data and reduces hallucinations.

A RAG pipeline has two stages: indexing and querying. During indexing, documents
are loaded, split into chunks, embedded, and stored in a vector database. During
querying, the user question is embedded, the most similar chunks are retrieved,
and the language model generates an answer conditioned on those chunks.

Chunking strategy matters a great deal. Chunks that are too large dilute the
relevant signal and waste context, while chunks that are too small lose
necessary surrounding meaning. A common approach uses a recursive character
splitter with a chunk size of several hundred tokens and an overlap of ten to
twenty percent to preserve continuity across boundaries.

The main advantages of RAG are that knowledge can be updated by changing the
documents rather than retraining the model, that answers can cite their sources,
and that private or domain-specific data can be used without fine-tuning. The
main limitations are that answer quality depends heavily on retrieval quality,
and that long retrieved contexts increase latency and cost.
