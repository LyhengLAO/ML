# Text Embeddings

An embedding is a dense vector representation of text in a continuous space where
semantic similarity corresponds to geometric proximity. Two sentences with
similar meaning are mapped to nearby vectors, even if they share no words. This
property is what makes embeddings the foundation of semantic search.

Modern embedding models are usually based on Transformer encoders fine-tuned with
contrastive learning. Sentence-Transformers models such as all-MiniLM-L6-v2
produce 384-dimensional vectors and offer an excellent balance between speed and
quality, which makes them popular for local, API-free retrieval systems.

Similarity between embeddings is most commonly measured with cosine similarity,
which compares the angle between two vectors and ignores their magnitude. A
cosine similarity of 1 means identical direction, 0 means orthogonal, and -1
means opposite. Euclidean distance and dot product are also used depending on
whether vectors are normalized.

A key practical detail is that the same embedding model must be used to encode
both the documents and the queries. Mixing different embedding models breaks the
shared geometry and destroys retrieval quality. Embeddings are typically computed
once for the corpus, stored in a vector database, and reused across many queries.
